Reading attention in brain waves

A 1D convolutional network that classifies children's EEG recordings as ADHD or control — trained and tested on separate groups of children, so the result reflects whether the pattern generalises to new people.

Not a diagnostic tool. ADHD is a clinical diagnosis made by professionals, never from a short EEG. This project classifies research-cohort group membership and is purely educational. Read the full disclaimer.

DeploymentONNX in the browser demo; TFLite in the Android app

The one decision that makes or breaks this

There are only 121 children in this dataset, but hundreds of thousands of EEG windows. If you shuffle the windows and split them randomly, the same child lands in both training and test — and the model can score near-perfectly simply by learning to recognise each child's individual brain signature, learning nothing about ADHD at all. A striking amount of published work on this exact dataset does precisely that.

So here the split is by subject: the 24 test children share no data with training, and even the validation set used during training is a separate group of children. Every number below is generalisation to new people.

Progress across iterations

The chart tracks validation accuracy (on held-out children) across training. It climbs from chance toward ~90% and holds — and crucially, the held-out test subjects land in the same range, so the model is reading something real about the groups rather than memorising individuals.

The honest reading of 92%

On the 24 unseen test subjects the model reaches 91.7% subject-level accuracy and a 0.965 ROC-AUC. That is a strong result — and notably, unlike the ECG project, there was no dramatic collapse from validation to test, which suggests the ADHD-vs-control EEG signature in this task really does transfer across children.

But two caveats keep it honest. First, 91.7% of 24 subjects is 22 of 24 — a small denominator with a wide confidence interval; the true accuracy could be meaningfully higher or lower. Second, and more important: this classifies which research group a recording came from. It is not a diagnosis, the cohort is small and specific, and real-world attention difficulties are far more varied than any single study captures.

What's next (v2)

The interesting follow-ups are about trust, not just accuracy: band-limit the input to see which frequencies carry the signal, ablate electrodes to find which scalp regions matter, and run subject-level cross-validation so the headline number rests on all 121 children rather than one 24-subject split. Those results will appear in the table above.
