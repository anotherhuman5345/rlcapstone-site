Reading ADHD in brain waves

An AI that reads kids' brain-wave recordings and guesses whether each one came from the ADHD group or the non-ADHD group of a study. The trick is that I trained and tested it on totally separate kids, so the score shows if it actually works on new people.

Not a diagnosis tool. ADHD is diagnosed by doctors, never from a quick brain scan. This just tells apart two groups from a research study, and it's purely a school project. Here's the full disclaimer.

The quick version

DataNasrabadi EEG set — 61 kids with ADHD + 60 without, ages 7–12
Signal19 sensors on the scalp, recorded while the kids did an attention task
ModelA neural network that reads all 19 sensors — 193,000 numbers (775 KB)
ResultOn 24 new kids: 91.7% right per kid, separating score 0.965
Runs onRight in your browser, and in an Android app

The one decision that makes or breaks it

There are only 121 kids in this data, but hundreds of thousands of little snippets. If you shuffle the snippets and split them randomly, the same kid ends up in both piles — and the AI can score almost perfectly just by recognizing each kid's own brain pattern, while learning nothing about ADHD at all. A surprising amount of published work on this exact dataset does exactly that by accident.

So I split it by kid: the 24 test kids share nothing with training, and even the practice-check kids are a totally separate group. Every number below is the AI working on new people.

Watching it learn

The chart tracks how well it does on held-out kids while it trains. It climbs from random-guessing up to about 90% and stays there — and the important part is that the real test kids land in the same range, which means it's reading something real about the groups instead of just memorizing individual kids.

What 92% really means here

On the 24 new kids it got 91.7% right with a strong separating score of 0.965. That's a solid result — and unlike my ECG project, it did not collapse from practice to real testing. That suggests the brain-wave pattern really does carry over from one kid to another.

But two things keep me honest. First, 91.7% of 24 kids is really 22 out of 24 — small numbers, so the true accuracy could be higher or lower. And second, and more important: this only tells apart two research groups. It's not a diagnosis, it's one small specific study, and real attention issues are way more varied than any single study.

What's next (v2)

The next steps are more about trust than a higher score: check which brain-wave frequencies carry the signal, turn off different sensors to see which parts of the head matter, and test on all 121 kids in rotation so the headline number rests on everyone, not just one group of 24. Those results will show up in the table above.
