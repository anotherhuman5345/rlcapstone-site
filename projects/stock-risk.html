Predicting a stock's risk — and testing whether sentiment helps

A model that sorts a stock's coming week into Low, Medium or High volatility from its price history and the news sentiment around it — built mainly to answer one question honestly: does the sentiment actually add anything?

Why volatility, not direction

Predicting whether a stock goes up or down tomorrow is famously close to a coin flip. Predicting how much it will move — its volatility — is a different story, because volatility clusters: calm weeks tend to follow calm weeks and turbulent ones follow turbulent ones. That autocorrelation is real, learnable signal, which is why a simple model reaches 56% on three classes where chance is 33%.

Progress across iterations

The model trains in seconds and settles around 64% on the validation slice, dropping to 56% on the later, unseen test period — a normal, honest gap when markets move to a regime the model hasn't seen.

The honest headline: sentiment didn't help

The whole point of the project was the word "sentiment", so the result that matters is the comparison. Training the same model on price features only — no sentiment — scored 57.2%, actually a hair better than the 56.3% with sentiment included. The sentiment signal added nothing; if anything it added a little noise.

That is not a bug, and it is not a disappointment — it is the most useful thing the project found. A daily average of headline sentiment is a coarse, sparse signal, and by the time you already have rolling-volatility features, most of what's predictable about next week's turbulence is already on the table. Reporting the negative result honestly — instead of quietly dropping the ablation and claiming sentiment "works" — is the difference between a demo and an experiment.

Where the model struggles

The confusion is exactly where you'd expect: it reads calm (Low, 76% recall) and turbulent (High, 60%) weeks reasonably well, but the Medium middle bucket is hard (27%), because "medium" volatility looks like a blurred edge between the other two. That's an honest, interpretable failure mode rather than a mysterious one.

What's next (v2)

If sentiment is going to earn its place, it needs to be richer than a daily average: per headline embeddings rather than one number, a longer multi-year history spanning more market regimes, and a test of whether sentiment helps more for direction than for volatility. Those results will appear in the table above — including, honestly, if sentiment still doesn't help.
