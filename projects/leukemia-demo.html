Leukemia cell classifier — live demo

Pick a real blood-smear cell below. The model sorts it into one of four classes — Benign or one of three B-cell ALL stages — in your browser. Nothing is uploaded.

Before you try it — this cannot diagnose leukemia.
  • Diagnosing leukemia takes a haematologist, bone-marrow analysis, flow cytometry and genetics — never a single cell image.
  • This is a demonstration of image classification on a research dataset, and (as the write-up explains) its headline accuracy is inflated by how the data is split. Treat every output as a teaching example.
  • If you have any health concern, see a qualified clinician.

Pick a cell

Each thumbnail is a real peripheral-blood-smear image of one white cell from the held-out test set.

On this dataset the model looks near-perfect — which is exactly the thing to be suspicious of. The write-up explains why, and what the patient-split v2 will show.

What am I looking at?

Each image is a single white blood cell, stained and photographed under a microscope. The malignant classes (Early Pre-B, Pre-B, Pro-B) are maturation stages of the same B-cell leukaemia; the benign class is a non-cancerous look-alike. Telling them apart is subtle even for specialists — which is what makes it an interesting computer-vision problem, and why the honest evaluation matters so much.
