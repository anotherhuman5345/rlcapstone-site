Sorting leukemia cells from a blood photo

An AI that looks at a photo of one white blood cell and sorts it into a healthy look-alike or one of three stages of a blood cancer — plus a lesson in why a 99.8% score can be the least trustworthy number on this whole site.

Not a diagnosis tool. Real leukemia diagnosis needs doctors and lab tests like bone-marrow analysis. This project just sorts research photos and is purely for learning. Here's the full disclaimer.

The four types

Three of the types (Early Pre-B, Pre-B, and Pro-B) are stages of the same blood cancer (ALL), and the fourth is a healthy look-alike that can fool your eye. Telling them apart is genuinely hard even for experts, which is what makes it a cool problem for an AI.

Watching it learn

The model picks up the four cell types fast and cleanly, and its practice score marches right up to 100%. On most projects that would be the big win. Here it's the opposite — it's the sign to stop and look harder at how I split the data.

Why 99.8% is a warning, not a win

The photos come from real patients, but this dataset doesn't tell you which patient each cell came from. So when I split it randomly, cells from the same patient almost certainly ended up in both the training and test piles. Cells from one slide share the same coloring, lighting, and background — and a strong AI can score almost perfectly by recognizing those things, while actually learning way less about the cancer than the number makes it look.

This is the same trap my brain-wave project avoided by splitting by kid. I couldn't avoid it here, because the patient info just isn't in the data — and pretending it wasn't a problem would be the dishonest move. So I report the 99.8% with an asterisk about as big as the number itself.

What's next (v2)

The next version uses a bigger dataset called C-NMC that does tell you which patient each cell came from — about fifteen thousand cells. That lets me keep each patient's cells together, exactly like I did for the brain waves, and finally see what the AI really learned. The score will almost certainly drop below 99.8% — and that lower number will be worth way more than this one. It'll show up in the table above when it's done.
