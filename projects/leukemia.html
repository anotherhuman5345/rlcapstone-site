Subtyping leukemia from a single cell

A computer-vision classifier that sorts white blood cells from a smear into Benign or one of three B-cell ALL stages — and a case study in why a 99.8% score can be the least trustworthy result on this whole site.

Not a diagnostic tool. Leukaemia is diagnosed by haematologists using bone-marrow biopsy, flow cytometry and genetics. This project classifies research images and is purely educational. Read the full disclaimer.

At a glance

TaskClassify one blood-smear white cell into 4 classes: Benign, Early Pre-B, Pre-B, Pro-B ALL
DataALL image dataset (Aria et al., 2021) — 3,256 peripheral-blood-smear images
ModelYOLO11s-cls, transfer-learned, 224×224 input (5.4M parameters)
Result99.8% accuracy, 0.998 macro-F1 on held-out images — see the caveat below
DeploymentONNX in the browser demo; TFLite in the Android app

The four classes

Three of the classes — Early Pre-B, Pre-B and Pro-B — are maturation stages of the same B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukaemia, and the fourth, Benign, is a non-cancerous look-alike (hematogones) that can fool the eye. Distinguishing them is genuinely subtle work, which is what makes it a worthwhile vision problem.

Progress across iterations

The model learns the four cell types quickly and cleanly, and validation accuracy marches to 100%. On most projects that would be the headline. Here it's the opposite — it's the cue to look harder at how the data was split.

Why 99.8% is a warning, not a win

The images come from a real set of patients, but this public release does not include patient identifiers. So when the data is split into training and test sets at random, the overwhelmingly likely outcome is that cells from the same patient land on both sides of the split. Cells from one slide share the same staining, lighting, focus and background — and a powerful model can score near-perfectly by recognising those cues, having learned far less about leukaemia than the number suggests.

This is the same trap the EEG project avoided by splitting on subjects. We can't avoid it here, because the identifiers simply aren't in the data — and pretending otherwise would be the dishonest move. So the 99.8% is reported with an asterisk the size of the result itself.

What's next (v2)

v2 moves to the C-NMC 2019 dataset, which ships patient IDs for roughly fifteen thousand cells. That lets us hold out whole patients, exactly as we did for EEG, and measure what the model has really learned. The near-certain outcome is a lower accuracy than 99.8% — and that lower number will be worth far more than this one. It will appear in the table above when it lands.
