MoleCheck: a phone app that checks moles

A phone app that looks at a photo of a mole and guesses whether it's probably harmless or worth getting checked. The whole thing runs on the phone — no server, no upload, works with no internet.

This is not a medical tool. MoleCheck is a school project that shows off image AI. It can't diagnose skin cancer or anything else. If you're worried about a mole or a skin change, see a real skin doctor. Here's the full disclaimer.

Why it runs on the phone

Photos of your skin are private. The easiest way to keep them private is to never send them anywhere: the AI lives inside the app, does all its thinking on the phone, and works even in airplane mode. Bonus — I didn't have to build or pay for any servers.

The data

I trained on a free collection of 11,720 real mole photos from the ISIC Archive (it includes the well-known HAM10000 set). Each photo is labeled harmless or dangerous. Most of them are harmless, which really shaped how I trained the model and how careful I made it (more on that below).

The model

The AI is called YOLO11, which already knew a lot about images before I started. I just fine-tuned it on moles for a bit on my own graphics card. I went with a solid, proven model instead of inventing my own, because for a project like this, something that works and is easy to put on a phone beats something fancy that's a pain to ship.

Making it cautious on purpose

A normal AI would use a 50/50 cutoff for its guesses, but that's wrong here. Missing a real cancer is way worse than a false alarm — a false alarm just means someone gets a mole checked that turns out fine. So I tuned it to lean toward "get it checked." At its final setting it catches 90% of the dangerous moles while still correctly clearing about 75% of the harmless ones.

Watching it learn

The chart below shows the model getting better as it trained. Here's a sneaky detail: at the very start it scores about 80% — which sounds good until you realize that just guessing "harmless" every time also scores 80%, because most moles are harmless. The learning that actually matters is everything above that dashed line.

The annoying technical part

To get the model onto a phone I had to convert it into a phone-friendly format. The easy one-step way only works on Mac and Linux, and I'm on Windows — so I had to take a longer route through a couple of other formats to get there.

I didn't just assume it still worked after all that converting. I tested the converted model against the original, and they matched almost exactly (within 0.001), so nothing important got lost. There were also a couple of tiny settings in the app that had to match the model perfectly, or it would quietly give nonsense answers — the kind of bug that takes forever to find.

The app

The Flutter app lets you take or pick a mole photo, runs the AI right there on the phone, and shows the result as a heads-up — never a diagnosis. The first time you open it, you have to tap through a disclaimer, and every result screen reminds you to see a real doctor.

What it can't do

  • It learned on special close-up medical photos, which look different from normal phone pics — so it'll probably do worse on real photos than the scores suggest.
  • The photos it learned from don't include enough darker skin tones, so it may not work equally well for everyone — a real fairness problem in skin AI.
  • Catching 90% still means it misses about 1 in 10 dangerous moles — which is exactly why it should never replace an actual doctor.
